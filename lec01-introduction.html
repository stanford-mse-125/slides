Excess Readmission Ratio

ERR = predicted readmissions / expected readmissions

above 1.0 → more readmissions than expected → penalty

Show this AFTER students have seen the histogram in the notebook. They’ve already formed intuitions about the distribution. Now the definition lands.

your hospital’s ERR is 1.05. why might your readmissions be so high? what questions would you ask, or what data would you gather, to understand why — and to figure out what you might do to lower them?

DISCUSSION: Think-pair-share (7 min — 2 min think, 3 min share, 2 min class). If stuck: “What kinds of patients does your hospital serve? What happens after they’re discharged? Is the expected rate adjusted for your patient population?” Key insight: Reducing ERR requires prediction (which patients are at risk?) and intervention (what can we change?). This is not just inference — it’s a decision problem.

which patients are most likely to be in the “too few” category? what does that mean for fairness?

DISCUSSION: Think-pair-share (7 min — 2 min think, 3 min share, 2 min class). If stuck: “Think about which hospitals have small sample sizes. Rural? Specialty? Serving underrepresented populations?” Key insight: Data that’s missing is not random — it’s systematically missing for the most vulnerable patients. Any analysis that ignores this is biased.

four ways to reason with data

same dataset, four different questions:

question decision
summary what does the ERR distribution look like? which hospitals are outliers?
prediction given a hospital’s traits, what ERR to expect? should CMS flag this hospital?
inference is an ERR of 1.05 real or noise? should the hospital be fined?
causation do fines actually reduce readmissions? should CMS continue the program?

This is the intellectual core of the restructured lecture. Walk through each row. “Same data — completely different questions. Summary tells you what happened. Prediction tells you what to expect. Inference tells you whether to believe it. Causation tells you what to do about it.” Connect to the acts: “Act 1 teaches summary and prediction. Act 2 teaches inference. Act 3 teaches causation.”

If a TA asks “why did you do it that way?”, you must be able to answer. data, model, decisions

many fields study the how to make consequential decisions with data:

  • operations research
  • management science
  • AI
  • machine learning
  • applied statistics
  • data science . . .

each has its own methods, tools, and culture. they overlap significantly!

These are all overlapping fields. The course draws from all of them. “Consequential decisions” is the throughline — not the methods, not the tools.

what makes a decision consequential?

  • financial — $881M loss (Zillow), $50B managed (Wealthfront)
  • human — who gets bail, who gets the vaccine
  • irreversible — can’t un-sentence, can’t un-approve, can’t un-elect
  • scale — 2M people fed, 200M recommendations served

every example in the montage is a consequential decision

This is the throughline. Connect each bullet back to a montage example. “Consequential” doesn’t just mean “big” — it means the cost of being wrong is high, and someone has to live with the outcome.

  • Financial: Zillow lost $881M because its pricing model was wrong. Wealthfront manages $50B — a bug in tax-loss harvesting costs real people real money.
  • Human: COMPAS scores determine bail — wrong score, someone sits in jail who shouldn’t. Pfizer vaccine — wrong call on efficacy, millions don’t get vaccinated (or get a bad vaccine). Hospital penalties — wrong readmission estimate, a hospital serving vulnerable patients loses funding.
  • Irreversible: You can’t un-sentence someone. You can’t un-approve a drug. Surgical decisions, infrastructure investments — some choices are one-way doors.
  • Scale: WFP feeds 2M people — reallocation affects every one of them. Netflix serves 200M users — a recommendation algorithm shapes what the whole world watches.
