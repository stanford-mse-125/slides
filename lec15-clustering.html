bridge from PCA

PCA k-means

The bridge slide. PCA (last lecture) found low-dimensional directions — every row got a continuous PC1, PC2 score. K-means today finds groups — every row gets one discrete cluster label.

Both are unsupervised — no y column, no “right answer” to predict. Both rely on Euclidean distance, so both need standardization for the same reason.

There’s also a deeper connection (mathematically, both approximate the data matrix in low rank — PCA with continuous coordinates, k-means with one-hot indicators) but that’s a connection for a more advanced class, not for slide 5.

About 60 sec.

k-means in four steps

k-means clustering

partition n points into k groups by repeating:

  1. assign each point to the nearest centroid
  2. recompute each centroid as the mean of its assigned points

until assignments stop changing

  • centroid — the mean of all points in a cluster
  • starts from k random centroids
  • converges in finite steps (assignments are discrete; objective only goes down)

The algorithm in a definition box. Two operations: assign points to centroids, recompute centroids from their assigned points. Iterate.

The two glosses: - centroid = cluster mean (one per cluster) - finite convergence — there are finitely many ways to assign n points to k clusters, and each step strictly decreases (or holds) the objective. So we can’t cycle forever.

About 60 sec.

watch it iterate

The 2D viz from the chapter — clustering Airbnb on just price and number of reviews (standardized) so we can plot it. k=3. Three colors are three clusters; the X markers are the centroids.

Panel 1: random initial centroids. Panel 2-3: centroids move toward the centers of their assigned points. Panel 4: stable — assignments have basically stopped changing.

The motion is the algorithm. Each panel: every point picks its nearest X; then each X moves to the average of its new neighborhood. Repeat.

About 60 sec.

the objective

inertia (within-cluster sum of squares)

\text{inertia} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2

total squared distance from each point to its assigned centroid

  • each iteration strictly decreases inertia (or holds it)
  • objective is non-convex — different starts can land at different local minima

The formal objective. K-means minimizes the sum of squared distances from each point to its assigned centroid. C_k is the set of indices assigned to cluster k; \mu_k is that cluster’s centroid.

Two key properties: 1. Each step monotonically decreases inertia — that’s how we know it converges. The assignment step picks the nearest centroid (can’t increase any term); the recompute step uses the mean (which minimizes squared distance from a set of points). Both reduce inertia.

  1. Non-convex. Different random initial centroids can converge to different local minima. We’ll come back to this in block 2 — the algorithm is not deterministic across random seeds.

About 90 sec.

one more thing first — standardize

k-means uses Euclidean distance

→ a feature in dollars (range 10–10,000) dominates a feature in degrees latitude (range 40.5–40.9)

feature unstandardized range influence on distance
price 10–10,000 enormous
latitude 40.5–40.9 invisible

same lesson as PCA: standardize before any distance-based method

Standardization is essential for k-means — exactly the same reason as PCA (last lecture). Both use Euclidean distance; both let the largest-magnitude feature dominate if you don’t standardize.

The Airbnb concrete: price spans four orders of magnitude; latitude spans 0.4 degrees. Without standardization, all the “distance” between two listings is just their price difference. Latitude contributes essentially nothing.

If anyone asks why we don’t just scale price down — that’s exactly what standardization does, just symmetrically across all features. StandardScaler subtracts the mean and divides by the standard deviation of each column.

About 2 min.

choose k. check stability. neither will save you.

elbow + silhouette: the standard tools

elbow method

plot inertia vs. k; look for a bend where adding clusters stops helping much

silhouette score

for each point: how similar to its own cluster vs. the nearest other cluster

range [-1, 1]: near +1 = sits comfortably in its cluster, 0 = on a boundary, -1 = probably misassigned

Two definitions stacked. Elbow: inertia always decreases as k grows (more clusters → smaller within-cluster distances), so we don’t pick on inertia directly — we pick on where it stops decreasing fast. Silhouette: a per-point quality score in [-1, 1], averaged across all points; the higher, the more cleanly separated.

These are the two heuristics every clustering tutorial reaches for. Watch what they say on real data.

About 90 sec.

elbow plot — no bend

  • no clear bend — inertia decreases steadily, typical of real data
  • silhouette peaks at k=2: just split the data in half
  • both are inconclusive

The two diagnostic plots from the chapter — elbow on the left, silhouette on the right.

Left (elbow): inertia drops smoothly from ~245k at k=2 down to ~120k at k=10. No bend. The annotation reads “no clear elbow — typical of real data.” This is what students will see on most real clustering problems: the curve is monotone and smooth, and the choice of k is arbitrary.

Right (silhouette): peaks at k=2 (~0.30), drops as k grows. The highest silhouette comes from the most trivial cluster structure: split into two halves.

Takeaway: on this dataset, the two standard heuristics give different answers, and neither is decisive. Choosing k is a judgment call, not a formula.

If asked: these heuristics aren’t useless — they can rule out clearly bad choices (e.g., silhouette score crashing toward 0 at k=15 tells you not to use 15). But they don’t pick k for you.

different seeds, different clusters

adjusted Rand index (ARI)

agreement between two clusterings, adjusted for chance

  • \text{ARI} = 1 → identical clusters
  • \text{ARI} = 0 → random agreement

so we can compare two runs of k-means with one number

The ARI is how we’ll quantify agreement between two clusterings. For any pair of points, ask: do both runs put them together, or split them apart?

ARI = 1 means the two runs partition every pair the same way (identical clusters, up to relabeling). ARI = 0 means the two runs agree no more than chance.

Compared to plain Rand index: ARI subtracts off the agreement you’d expect from randomly relabeling, so it’s a fair score even when one clustering is much more unbalanced than the other.

About 60 sec.

10 runs, pairwise ARI

  • most pairs agree perfectly (ARI = 1.00)
  • runs 3 and 7 are different clusterings (ARI ≈ 0.72, 0.76)
  • same data, same algorithm, same k → not the same clusters

The heatmap from the chapter. Each cell is the ARI between two of the 10 runs (with n_init=1, so each is one random start).

Most cells are dark red — ARI = 1.00, runs landed on identical clusters. But two rows pop: runs 3 and 7 sit at ARI ≈ 0.72 and 0.76 against every other run. They found different local minima.

This is real-life clustering: most runs converge to the same answer, a few find different solutions, and you can’t tell from the algorithm output which solution is the “right” one. There’s no global ranking — they’re all local optima.

About 90 sec.

sklearn’s defenses (and their limits)

# sklearn defaults:
KMeans(n_clusters=3, n_init=10, init='k-means++')
  • n_init=10 — runs k-means 10 times, keeps the lowest-inertia run
  • k-means++ — picks initial centroids that are spread apart, not random rows

helps. doesn’t fix it. different choices of random_state can still land on different solutions.

What sklearn does by default. n_init=10 is a major defense — out of 10 random starts, you keep the best one. k-means++ initialization picks initial centroids that are spread apart (algorithmic: pick the first centroid randomly; pick each subsequent centroid with probability proportional to squared distance to the nearest existing centroid). Together these dramatically reduce — but do not eliminate — sensitivity to the random seed.

The earlier heatmap used n_init=1 to expose the raw instability. With n_init=10, you’ll see fewer disagreements, but the underlying issue (multiple local minima) hasn’t gone away.

About 60 sec.

what does “Cluster 1” mean?

Warning

if your clusters change when the seed changes, the labels carry no portable meaning.

“Cluster 1” in one run is not “Cluster 1” in the next — and may not be the same set of points at all.

→ never report cluster labels ('1', '2', '3') — report profiles (“budget”, “moderate”, “premium”)

The interpretation gotcha. K-means doesn’t label the clusters — it just produces them. The integer labels are arbitrary; sklearn assigns them in the order the algorithm happens to discover the centroids.

Practical rule: never put cluster_id = 1 in a report. Profile the cluster (avg price, room type, location) and name it for what it captures. “Budget shared rooms” is portable; “Cluster 1” is not.

About 90 sec.

why did lat/lon dominate?

two features → spatial separation is strong and clean

a Manhattan listing and a Bronx listing are standard-deviations apart in (lat, lon)

every other feature gets drowned out

moral: if a feature carries clean geometric separation, it will dominate the cluster boundaries

Why lat/lon are so dominating. In NYC, the spatial spread (~0.4 degrees lat × 0.3 degrees lon) is large relative to typical within-borough variation, so once we standardize, a cross-borough listing pair is several standard deviations apart in (lat, lon). That swamps the more subtle separations in price, room type, etc.

The general principle: any feature whose values cleanly separate the data into geometric clumps will dominate k-means. ZIP code, neighborhood, even time-of-listing if the data spans years. These features can be useful for prediction but swamp clustering toward whatever they already encode.

genes mirror geography

PCA on 197,000 SNPs from 1,387 Europeans. no geographic info given to the algorithm.

it drew us a map of Europe

  • PC1 vs. latitude: r^2 = 0.71
  • PC2 vs. longitude: r^2 = 0.72
  • 50% of people placed within 310 km of birthplace

math result: PCA on spatial data generically produces these patterns when similarity decays with distance

Novembre et al., Nature 2008; Novembre & Stephens, Nature Genetics 2008

The canonical case. Novembre et al. 2008 ran PCA on ~197K SNPs across 1,387 individuals from across Europe. They handed the algorithm no geographic information whatsoever — just the genotypes. The first two principal components, when plotted, reproduced the map of Europe.

The temptation is to read this as “the algorithm discovered hidden population structure.” The same research community published the math showing otherwise: Novembre & Stephens 2008 (Nature Genetics) proved that PCA on any spatial data with distance-decaying similarity will produce gradient and sinusoidal patterns generically — no historical demographic event required. François et al. 2010 went further: under range-expansion simulations, PC1 can orient perpendicular to the true expansion direction.

So the famous Cavalli-Sforza interpretation of European PC1 as “Neolithic farmers walking west” is unidentified by this method — the gradient is built into PCA on spatial data, not into European prehistory.

Consequence: these maps are routinely misappropriated to support biological-race claims, which the actual authors explicitly reject. The “clusters” reflect geographic sampling structure and continuous clines, not discrete groups.

90-second story for class: “We gave the algorithm DNA, with zero geographic info. It drew us a map. Did it discover hidden populations? Or did it rediscover the obvious — your neighbors share more ancestry with you than strangers do? The math says the second.”

The bridge to lec15: same algorithm family (k-means and PCA both partition by Euclidean distance after standardization), same lesson. Feature set in → structure out, and the structure mostly reflects what we put in.

About 2 min.

predictive policing finds the police

Lum & Isaac, 2016 — PredPol replication on Oakland 2010 drug arrest data

predicted hotspots: West Oakland + International Blvd ~200× more drug arrests there than elsewhere

true drug use (NSDUH survey): roughly uniform across the city

black residents would be targeted at roughly twice the rate of whites — despite roughly even drug use

Lum & Isaac, Significance 2016

Same lesson, much higher stakes. Lum and Isaac reimplemented PredPol — a real predictive-policing product deployed in Santa Cruz, LAPD, and others — on Oakland’s 2010 drug arrest records. PredPol’s only feature was where past arrests happened.

The algorithm’s hotspots concentrated in West Oakland and along International Boulevard: about 200× the arrest density of “non-hotspot” areas. They then compared to estimated true drug use (from the National Survey on Drug Use and Health combined with Census demographics) — which is roughly uniform across Oakland.

The clusters traced arrest patterns, not crime patterns. Because that’s the feature the algorithm got. Feed the model “where we arrest people,” and it will tell you “arrest more people there.” The feedback loop closes itself: predict a hotspot → patrol there → more arrests → tomorrow’s model more confident.

Quantitative consequence the paper drove home: under PredPol, Black residents would face about 2× the policing exposure of white residents, residents of other non-white races about 1.5×, despite roughly even underlying drug use across groups.

Policy consequence: Santa Cruz banned predictive policing in 2020 (first US city), LAPD ended its contract that year. This paper is the central empirical citation in the AI fairness literature.

For class: “Same algorithm, two feature sets. Train on arrests, you get a map of where we already police. Train on a public-health survey, you get a map of where drugs are. Different maps. The choice between them is a policy decision in statistics clothing.”

About 2 min.

sixteen personality types — or four coin-flips at the median

MBTI assigns each test-taker to one of 16 “types” by binarizing four trait scores at zero.

Warning

the underlying scores are roughly normal, with the mode at the cutpoint

  • ~50% of takers get a different type on retest within weeks
  • $20M/yr industry; used by 89 of the Fortune 100

the “types” are the bars to the left and right of one arbitrary line through a bell curve

McCrae & Costa, J. Personality 1989; Bess & Harvey, J. Personality Assessment 2002

The cleanest demonstration of feature-induced clusters: the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator. MBTI assigns each test-taker to one of 16 personality types via four binary axes (E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P). The procedure is: score each axis on a continuous scale, then cut at zero. Above zero → letter A; below → letter B.

McCrae & Costa 1989 (J. of Personality) and Bess & Harvey 2002 (J. of Personality Assessment) showed: the underlying scores on each axis are roughly normally distributed, with the mode (most common score) sitting right at the cutoff. So the “types” are produced by cutting a unimodal continuum at its densest point — guaranteeing that small score perturbations re-classify the person. About 50% of test-takers get a different type on retest within a few weeks.

Bess & Harvey specifically debunked earlier “bimodality” reports as artifacts of BILOG default quadrature settings: when finer numerical settings were used, the score distributions became strongly center-weighted, not bimodal.

So MBTI’s 16 types are an imposed structure, not a discovered one. The clusters exist because the test designer wrote them in.

Consequence: ~$20M/year industry; used by 89 of the Fortune 100 in hiring, team-building, career counseling. Decisions made on the basis of a clustering structure the data doesn’t actually have.

Cross-domain pattern: in three independent fields — genetics, policing, psychology — the same lesson recurs because clustering’s first job is to mirror its inputs, not to discover something independent of them.

About 60 sec.

the clustering gotcha checklist

before reporting any cluster, ask:

  • what features did I include? — geography, IDs, dates often dominate
  • what features did I drop, and why? — feature choice is the model
  • is k defensible? — elbow + silhouette + business reason
  • are the clusters stable? — n_init=50, multiple seeds, ARI > 0.9
  • can I name each cluster from its profile? — if not, you have an artifact
  • does the clustering answer a question someone has? — if no, don’t ship

Six-item checklist. This is the chapter’s “what an uncritical analysis skips” list, reframed as the questions a careful analyst asks before reporting.

Order matters: features first (the biggest lever), then k, then stability, then interpretation, then decision-relevance. Each one is a single sentence that, if asked, would catch a common failure.
