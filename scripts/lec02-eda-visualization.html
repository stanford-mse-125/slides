The histogram (30 sec)

Slide: “the histogram tells a different story”

“Dramatic right skew. Most listings are $50-$200, but a few luxury listings pull the tail way out. Which single number represents ‘typical’?”

Mean vs. median definitions (2 min)

Slide: “mean vs. median”

“We saw the histogram. Now the vocabulary.” Reveal mean definition. “The arithmetic average. Sensitive to extreme values — those $999 listings are pulling it right.” Reveal median. “The middle value. Half below, half above. Barely moves when you add expensive outliers.”

Numerical Q/A (1 min)

Slide: “which summary do you trust?”

“Mean $152, median $106. If you price your listing at the mean — $152 — roughly what fraction of listings are cheaper than yours?” Give 15 seconds. “Well over half. The right tail pulls the mean above where most of the data lives. The median is a better measure of typical.”

Quartiles and IQR (1 min)

Slide: “quartiles and IQR”

“Quartiles divide the data into four equal parts. The IQR — Q3 minus Q1 — is the width of the middle 50%. It’s robust: extreme values barely change it. Mean and standard deviation are not robust.”

Log scale comparison (1 min)

Slide: “same data, different lens”

“Two views of the same data. On the raw scale, everything’s bunched at the left and the tail is invisible. On the log scale, the distribution looks roughly symmetric. The log scale doesn’t change the data — it changes the ruler.”

  2. Price vs. accommodates scatter: linear scale first (mess), then log y-axis (clear upward trend). “On the log scale, each additional guest roughly multiplies the price.”

DISCUSSION: Predict-then-reveal (3 min)

Slide: discussion slide — “sketch what you expect”

“I’m about to plot price on a log y-axis versus number of guests on the x-axis. Here’s the key question: does each additional guest add a fixed dollar amount — say $50 — or multiply the price by a factor? If it’s additive, what does that look like on a log scale? If it’s multiplicative?”

Give 1 min to sketch or discuss.

After 1 min: show the scatter plot from the notebook.

Debrief: “It’s roughly linear on the log scale — meaning the relationship is multiplicative. Each additional guest multiplies the price by a roughly constant factor, maybe 1.3×. If it were additive, the curve would flatten on the log scale. The spread also increases — bigger groups have more price variability.”

Block 3: Missing data (20 min total)

Dark slide (15 sec)

Slide: “what’s missing?”

Cinematic pause.

The 300 missing hospitals (1 min)

Slide: “the 300 missing hospitals”

“Remember block 1? df.describe() counted 2,800 values, but there were 3,100 rows. Those 300 rows are hospitals whose readmission counts were ‘Too Few to Report.’ Let’s find out who they are.”

Suppression by condition (2 min)

Slide: “suppression varies by condition”

“CMS suppresses the count when it’s too small. But the rates are wildly different: CABG — coronary artery bypass — about 50% suppressed. Heart failure — only about 8%.” Pause. “Which hospitals are most likely to have small counts?” Wait 5-10 seconds. “Small hospitals. Rural hospitals. Specialty centers. Hospitals with low patient volume for that condition.”

Three patterns of missingness (4 min)

Slides: MCAR → MAR → MNAR (one slide each)

MCAR (1 min): “Missing completely at random. No pattern. Example: a lab tech drops 10% of blood samples. The destroyed samples aren’t systematically different from the rest. Safe to ignore — you lose power but not accuracy.”

MAR (1 min): “Missing at random — conditional on something we observe. Example: older patients skip a survey question, but we know their age. The missingness has a pattern, but we can see the pattern. We can sometimes correct for it.”

MNAR (2 min): “Missing not at random. The value itself determines whether you see it. Example: patients who had complications don’t respond to the follow-up survey. The outcome we want to measure is exactly what drives the missingness.” Pause. “This is the most dangerous pattern. Dropping these rows introduces bias — a systematic error that doesn’t shrink with more data.”

Classification Q/A (1 min)

Slide: “classify this”

“The CMS data suppresses hospitals with small readmission counts. MCAR, MAR, or MNAR?” Give 10 seconds. Some students will say MAR. Push back: “Hospital size is observed, yes. But the censoring threshold is on the readmission count itself — the value we’re trying to measure. That’s MNAR.”

MNAR simulation sequence (3 min)

Slides: three simulation plots

Plot 1 — all hospitals: “Let’s build a toy example where we know the truth. 20 simulated hospitals. Small hospitals on the left — slightly higher, noisier readmission rates. The line marks the true mean.”

Plot 2 — censored: “Now suppress the 5 smallest hospitals. Grayed out — ‘Too Few to Report.’ The dashed line is the true mean. The solid red line is the reported mean — computed only from the 15 visible hospitals. The mean shifted down.”

Plot 3 — histograms: “Side by side. All 20 hospitals on the left. After censoring on the right. The distribution shifts. The reported mean underestimates the true mean. This is MNAR bias.”

Return to real data (30 sec)

Slide: “back to the real data”

“This is exactly what’s happening in the CMS data. But in the real world, we can’t peek behind the curtain. We know the bias exists. We can reason about its direction. But we can’t measure how big it is without assumptions.”

DISCUSSION: Think-pair-share (5 min)

Slide: discussion slide — “does the reported average overestimate or underestimate?”

Read the setup aloud: “CMS averages ERR across reporting hospitals to set the penalty threshold. The 300 suppressed hospitals are disproportionately small and rural. Suppose their ERR tends to be above 1.0.” Then the question: “Does the reported average overestimate or underestimate the true national average? Who gets hurt?”

“One minute on your own.”

After 1 min: “Talk to your neighbor.”

After 2 more min: “Let’s hear it.” Take 2-3 responses.

If a student challenges the premise: “Good catch — ERR adjusts for patient severity. But it doesn’t adjust for hospital resources, staffing, or follow-up programs. Small hospitals may lack those, which is what drives ERR above 1.0 even after the patient-mix adjustment.”

If stuck: “If the missing hospitals would pull the average up, and they’re excluded, which way is the reported average biased?”

Debrief: “The reported average is too low. The penalty threshold, calibrated to this biased average, may be too lenient. And the hospitals most likely to need support — the small, rural ones — are the ones we can’t see.”

Confounding teaser (3 min)

Dark slide (15 sec)

Slide: “association is not causation”

Office hours example (1.5 min)

Slide: “students who attend office hours get higher grades”

“Students who attend office hours get higher grades. How much of the grade boost is from office hours — the practice, the feedback — and how much is from the kind of student who shows up?” Give 10 seconds.

The confounder (1 min)

Slide: “the confounder”

“Student motivation is the confounder. Motivated students go to office hours and study hard independently. Office hours probably do help — you get practice, feedback, clarification. But the raw correlation overstates how much. If the university mandated attendance, the grade boost would be smaller than the correlation suggests.” Pause. “When you see an association, always ask: what else could drive both variables? We’ll formalize this idea later in the course.”
