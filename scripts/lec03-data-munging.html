Discussion 1: PrivacySuppressed bias (5 min)

Slide: discussion — “if you drop those rows…”

“Think for one minute: if you drop the PrivacySuppressed rows, what kind of schools remain? Would the average earnings go up or down?”

After 1 min think + 2 min pair: take 2-3 responses.

If stuck: “Think about which schools have small cohorts — rural? specialized? under-resourced?”

Key insight: Dropping these rows removes small, rural, specialized schools. The remaining sample is biased toward large institutions. Average earnings probably goes up — small schools tend to serve lower-earning populations. This is selection bias.

Missingness is not random (1 min)

Slide: bar chart — suppression by size

“Nearly a third of the smallest schools are suppressed. Among 2,000+ students: about 1%. This isn’t random.”

MCAR/MAR/MNAR (2 min)

Slide: “three patterns of missingness”

Show definition box. “MCAR: truly random — mold destroys random records. MAR: depends on things we can see — older patients skip surveys, and we have their age. MNAR: depends on the missing value itself — the sickest patients don’t report, and we never observe their outcomes.”

Advance to reveal: “College Scorecard earnings: MNAR. Small cohort means suppressed. Small schools are systematically different.”

Vivid example from the book: “In ambulance data, a missing heart rate often means the EMTs were too busy saving the patient. The missing value is the signal.”

Block 2: Joining datasets (18 min total)

Dark slide (5 sec)

Slide: “block 2: joining datasets”

Why join? (1 min)

Slide: “why join?”

“The Scorecard comes in two files. Institution-level: one row per school. Program-level: one row per school-program pair.” Advance to reveal the questions. “To answer richer questions, we need to combine them.”

Inner join vs. left join (3 min)

Slides: toy example → inner join → left join (3 slides)

Show the Students and Majors tables side by side. “Before I show you the result — which students survive an inner join on major_id?” Take quick answers.

Advance to inner join result. “Alice and Bob survive. Carol is gone — no match for 103. History is gone — no match for 104. Neither table warns you.”

Advance to left join. “All three students survive. Carol’s major becomes NaN — a signal that she has no matching major. That signal is lost in an inner join.”

One-to-many (2 min)

Slide: “one-to-many: row multiplication”

Show left and right tables. “Two rows on the left, three on the right, all with key A.” Pause. “How many rows in the result?”

Advance to reveal. “Six. 2 × 3 = 6. Joins can GROW your data, not just shrink it.”

Defense: check your key (1 min)

Slide: “defense: check your key before joining”

Show the assert. “One line of code. An assert that passes is documentation. An assert that fails is an alarm.”

Discussion 2: Predict join row count (5 min)

Slide: discussion — “predict: how many rows…”

“The scorecard has 7,703 institutions. Programs has 228,000 rows across 5,500 institutions. How many rows will the inner join produce? More or fewer than 7,703?”

Most students will say “fewer” — they’re thinking of inner joins as filters. The answer is ~216,000 — a massive expansion because each institution matches to many programs.

If stuck: “Each institution matches to many programs. Stanford alone has 180+.”

Key insight: The inner join is one-to-many — it expands, not contracts. Students who predicted “fewer” were thinking about key overlap (correct for one-to-one joins) but missed the one-to-many expansion.

Demo: join explosion (3 min)

Slide: Demo slide

Switch to notebook. Show: 1. The assert on UNITID uniqueness 2. The inner join: 7,703 → ~216,000 rows 3. The left join: preserves all 7,703 institutions 4. The bar chart comparing row counts

“7,700 rows became 216,000. And neither join warned you.”

After every join (2 min)

Slide: “after every join: check row counts”

Walk through the table. “Inner joins can gain AND lose rows at the same time — one-to-many expansion plus unmatched key dropping. Left joins gain rows but don’t lose them.”

Advance to reveal: “The join type is a decision, not a default.”

Block 3: When tools betray you (25 min total)

Dark slide (5 sec)

Three approaches to missing earnings (3 min)

Slide: “three approaches to missing earnings”

Advance to reveal: “If they all give the same mean, why does it matter?”

Discussion 3: AI dropna trust (7 min)

Slide: discussion — “an AI calls .dropna()…”

“An AI ‘cleans’ the Scorecard by calling dropna and reports average earnings of $45,000. Name two questions you’d ask, focused on what was lost in the cleaning step.”

If stuck: “How many rows survived? Are the dropped rows systematically different? What was the missingness rate?”

Key insight: The questions to ask: (1) How many rows survived — what fraction of the data was lost? (2) Are the dropped schools systematically different — were small/for-profit schools disproportionately dropped? (3) Which approach was used and why?

Stanford case study (3 min)

Slide: “Stanford: a case study in hidden complexity”

“Median earnings: $86,000. SAT average: 1465. Sounds good?”

Advance to reveal: “$86K qualifies as ‘Low Income’ in Santa Clara County. The threshold for one person is $111,700.”

Advance to the three factors: “Title IV only — many Stanford students don’t take federal aid. 10 years after enrollment — includes years in grad school. All fields pooled — CS at $139K, English at $31K.”

“The gap between the Scorecard number and your expectations is itself a data-cleaning lesson.”

Type coercion (2 min)

Slide: “type coercion: tools that corrupt data”

“pd.to_numeric with errors=‘coerce’ converts PrivacySuppressed to NaN. No warning, no count.” Show the defense code. “Always check what you lost.”

Sentinel values (1 min)

Slide: “sentinel values”

Quick. “-999 looks like a number. Mean WITH sentinels: -208°F. Mean WITHOUT: 72.9°F. Unlike NaN, sentinel values participate in every computation.”

Identifier trap (1 min)

Slide: “the identifier trap”

Quick. “OPEID with leading zeros — stored as integer, zeros vanish. Later join against zero-padded strings: zero matches, no error. ZIP codes, phone numbers, IDs: codes, not numbers.”

Discussion 4: ChatGPT ROI ranking (5 min)

Slide: discussion — “you upload the Scorecard to ChatGPT…”

“ChatGPT ranks schools by raw MD_EARN_WNE_P10 after dropping all missing data. Stanford is #47. Small groups: identify at least two problems.”

If stuck: “How many rows survived the dropna? What does MD_EARN_WNE_P10 actually measure — and who’s in the sample?”

Key insight: (1) Raw earnings confounded by selectivity — high-SAT schools admit students who’d earn well anywhere. (2) Dropping rows loses most small/for-profit schools. (3) The metric pools all fields and measures 10yr after enrollment. (4) May not be reproducible across sessions.
