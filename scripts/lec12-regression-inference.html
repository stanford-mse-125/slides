Lecture 12: Regression Inference + Diagnostics — Facilitation Script

Duration: 80 minutes Total slides: 41 (slide 1 = auto title from YAML) Primary datasets: Airbnb NYC listings (14,689 entire-apt listings under $500/night) and NBA player-game data (72,156 player-games, REST_DAYS ≤ 7) Hook: parallel decision problems — Airbnb host considering a $50K bathroom renovation; NBA front office considering schedule restructuring around rest. Same regression machinery, very different verdicts. Structure: Opening (3 min, slides 1–4) → Block 1: Airbnb regression (~22 min, slides 5–15) → Block 2: NBA cautionary tale (~22 min, slides 16–26) → Block 3: diagnostics (~22 min, slides 27–35) → Logistic coda (~7 min, slides 36–38) → Closing (4 min, slides 39–41) → Buffer (~0 min)

Opening (3 min) — slides 1–4

Slide 1 (title): 30 seconds. Date, name.

Slide 2 (logistics): START RECORDING. 30 seconds. HW 3 due Friday — uses regression inference directly. Project proposals were submitted last week; feedback returned next week.

Slide 3 (the brief): Frame the lecture as two parallel decision problems. Your friend is an Airbnb host considering a $50K renovation; another friend at an NBA front office wants to restructure around rest. Same toolkit will give very different answers. Don’t tease the punchlines — the bathroom CI lands in block 1, the rest effect-size in block 2.

Slide 4 (today): Four beats: Airbnb regression (read the table), NBA cautionary tale, diagnostics, logistic coda. Each block uses the same template established in block 1.

Block 1: Airbnb regression (~22 min) — slides 5–15

Key narrative

Establish the canonical regression-table reading template. Walk through the bathrooms coefficient end to end (sign, SE, t, p, CI). The decision-grade output: bathroom premium $59–$66/night, payback ~3 years.

Slide 5 (dark block marker). “regression for decisions.”

Slide 6 (load + filter): ~1 min. Filter to entire-apt listings under $500 with bathroom/bedroom data — 14,689 listings. The next slide shows what they look like.

Slide 7 (price by bathrooms and borough): ~1 min. Boxplots show the obvious patterns: more bathrooms → higher price; Manhattan > Brooklyn > rest. But the bathrooms picture is confounded by bedrooms (more rooms → more of both). Multivariate regression isolates the bathroom premium.

Slide 8 (DISCUSSION: predict before you fit): 45 sec inline predict. Students write down guesses for (1) bathrooms coefficient and (2) Manhattan-vs-Bronx gap. Most will guess $50–$100 for each; both are in that ballpark.

Slide 9 (the regression table): ~2 min. Display model.summary(). Don’t try to teach all eight elements at once — point at intercept, borough rows (gap relative to Bronx), bathrooms ($62.44), bedrooms ($34). The next slide makes the column structure explicit.

Slide 10 (reading the table): ~3 min. Five columns per row + header (R², F) + footer (Cond. No.). Walk through bathrooms: $62.44 (coef), $1.86 (SE), 33.6 (t), <0.001 (p), [$58.80, $66.09] (CI). R² = 0.40, Cond. No. = 35 (just over the multicollinearity threshold; bathrooms ↔︎ bedrooms expected). F-statistic in the Going-deeper note — not used in this course.

Slide 11 (interpreting the coefficients): ~2 min. Read the signs. Bathroom premium ≈ $62. Bedroom $34. Manhattan +$79 vs Bronx. Brooklyn +$26 vs Bronx. Queens, Staten Island not significantly different from Bronx. The “holding everything else constant” gloss is what students should understand by “controlling for.”

Slide 12 (DISCUSSION: would adding a bedroom raise YOUR price by $34?): 4 minutes. 1 min think + 2 min pair + 1 min debrief. Get students to see that the regression coefficient is an across-listings association, not a personal intervention prediction. Adding a bedroom usually means a bigger apartment — many things change at once. Key insight: an across-listings association is not a counterfactual claim about your unit.

Slide 13 (the conditional null): ~2 min. H₀ is conditional, not unconditional. The same predictor can fail in one model and pass in another. Students saw exactly this in Ch 5 — the bathrooms coefficient was bigger before bedrooms entered. Critical for block 2: the NBA REST_DAYS test is “does rest add anything beyond player quality, opponent strength, home/away?”

Slide 14 (the t-statistic): ~2 min. Gosset callback to Ch 10. When you estimate the SE from data, the ratio has heavier tails — Student’s t. n is large here (~15,000) so t* ≈ 1.96. Multiple-testing aside (Lec 11): a regression with p predictors runs p tests at once; with the p-values seen here (<0.001) Bonferroni doesn’t change conclusions, but for marginal p-values it would.

Slide 15 (CI for bathrooms): ~2 min. Plug in: 62.44 ± 1.96 × 1.86 = [$58.80, $66.09]. Bootstrap on 1,000 resamples agrees ([$58.78, $66.04]). The decision-grade output. Width ~$7 is tight enough to use; lower bound $59 clears the renovation hurdle.

Slide 16 (decision: invest $50K?): ~2 min. $59–$66/night × 256 nights/year (70% occupancy × 365) = ~$15K–$17K/year. Payback ~3 years. Yes, the data support the renovation — with two caveats: (a) association ≠ intervention, and (b) we need to verify the diagnostics (block 3).

Block 2: NBA cautionary tale (~22 min) — slides 17–27

Key narrative

Run the same machinery on a question where the data refuse to confirm the prior. REST_DAYS is “significant” at p < 0.001 with n = 72,000, but Cohen’s d ≈ -0.02. Significance ≠ importance. Then back up to Simpson’s paradox: even the small effect is suspect because of confounding.

Slide 17 (dark block marker). “same machinery, different verdict.”

Slide 18 (the NBA question): ~1 min. Setup the confounding sketch: bench players, stars, injured players all have non-random rest patterns. The four-predictor model: REST_DAYS + PLAYER_SEASON_AVG + HOME + OPP_GS_ALLOWED.

Slide 19 (fit, then read): ~2 min. Display model.summary(). REST_DAYS row: -0.15, p < 0.001, CI [-0.20, -0.11]. Don’t reveal the punchline — let students sit with “the data say rest hurts performance, p < 0.001” for a moment.

Slide 20 (but check the size): ~3 min. Cohen’s d definition: coefficient divided by response SD. For REST_DAYS: -0.15 / 7.85 = -0.0195 SD. Essentially zero. Caveat: Cohen’s benchmarks are conventions, not laws. Domain context overrides.

Slide 21 (significance ≠ importance): ~2 min. The pivotal table. NBA REST_DAYS and Airbnb bathrooms both clear every textbook bar (p < 0.001, CI excludes 0). Cohen’s d is 15× larger for bathrooms. Practically meaningful: yes for Airbnb, no for NBA. The aphorism caps the block.

Slide 22 (DISCUSSION: questions to ask the analytics team): 5 minutes. 1 min think + 2 min pair + 2 min debrief. Target answers: (1) how big is the effect, (2) is there confounding, (3) does the model capture all relevant factors, (4) would this hold out of sample. Push for #3 and #4. Key insight: a tight CI does not protect you from any of these — CIs widen with sample noise, not with confounding.

Slide 23 (standardized coefficients): ~1 min. Bar chart with 95% CIs. Player quality dominates; REST_DAYS barely clears zero. The bar that should drive a strategic decision is invisible.

Slide 24 (variable names can deceive): ~2 min. The OPP_GS_ALLOWED warning. We named the variable mean GS allowed by the opponent, so high = porous defense. The positive coefficient is mechanical. Lesson: a coefficient’s sign is only interpretable if you know how the variable was constructed. Real-world failure mode.

Slide 25 (confounding made visible): ~3 min. Simpson’s-paradox-in-regression visualization. Aggregate slope (dashed) is steeper than within-quartile slopes (colored). Adding PLAYER_SEASON_AVG = switching from the dashed line to the colored ones. Strictly, this is the attenuation form of Simpson’s, not a sign-flip.

Slide 26 (association, not causation): ~2 min. Coaches choose when to rest — DNP after a soft loss differs from DNP before a back-to-back. Even with all measured controls, the rest coefficient mixes the rest effect with strategic-scheduling effects. Forward to Ch 18: DAGs.

Block 3: diagnostics (~22 min) — slides 27–35

Key narrative

Same template, sharper purpose. LINE conditions checked via residual plot (L, E), Q-Q plot (N), and — for I — by asking whether rows are clustered. Both models show mild heteroscedasticity and right-skew. Coefficient inference survives via CLT; prediction intervals are dicier. The chapter’s strongest sentence: diagnostics catch statistical, not systematic, problems.

Slide 27 (dark block marker). “is the inference trustworthy?”

Slide 28 (LINE conditions): ~2 min. Definition box. Each letter has a signature. Independence is hardest to check from residuals alone — both datasets cluster (NBA repeats players; Airbnb clusters by host). Clustered SEs are the standard fix; we leave it to homework. Reassure: with n in the tens of thousands, clustering would widen CIs but not flip “significant vs not” calls.

Slide 29 (residual plot — Airbnb): ~2 min. Spread fans out as fitted price grows — textbook funnel. Mild heteroscedasticity (E violation). Standard fix: log-transform price.

Slide 30 (residual plot — NBA): ~2 min. Symmetric around zero in residuals-vs-fitted, but scale-location LOWESS rises 0.2 → 1.0. Same E-violation, different form: low-projection players have less room to deviate; high-projection stars vary more game-to-game.

Slide 31 (Q-Q plot): ~2 min. Both Airbnb and NBA show right-skew (right tail bows above the line). Airbnb: prices have a hard floor at $0 but a long right tail. NBA: biggest games (12-for-15 from three) happen more often than a normal would predict.

Slide 32 (Q-Q gallery): ~1 min. Reference figure. Six distributions paired with their Q-Q plots. Each shape (concave, convex, S, reverse-S, gap) maps to a residual pattern. Don’t dwell — students should just see “right-skew = convex bowing up” matches both our examples.

Slide 33 (PI vs CI for the mean): ~2 min. Two intervals, two questions. Blue band: CI for the mean — narrow. Orange band: prediction interval — wide. Where data is dense (near 1 bathroom), the PI brackets most of the gray scatter — exactly the job of a 95% PI. The red shaded zone marks where the band is extrapolated beyond the data.

Slide 34 (diagnostics catch statistical, not systematic): ~3 min. The chapter’s strongest single sentence. NBA REST_DAYS is the canonical case — tight CI, clean diagnostics, but the coefficient is the wrong answer to a causal question because rest assignment is correlated with unmeasured strategic factors. Confounders bias the point estimate; they don’t show up in the SE. Bridge to Ch 18.

Slide 35 (DISCUSSION: spot the LINE violation): 5 minutes. 30 sec think + 2 min pair + 2 min debrief. Diagnostic exercise: regression of monthly health-insurance claim cost; Q-Q plot is convex with right tail bowing up. N is failing — right-skew, common in cost data because of catastrophic claims. Standard fix: log-transform the response. Coefficients become “fractional change per unit predictor.” Probe: would clustering help? No, that’s I. Would adding predictors help? Possibly L, not N directly. (If running short on time, skip this discussion.)

Logistic coda (~7 min) — slides 36–38

Key narrative

Same template, just z replaces t. Coefficients exponentiate to odds ratios.

Slide 36 (dark transition). “the same template, with z.”

Slide 37 (logistic regression: same recipe): ~2 min. Two-column comparison: linear vs logistic. t→z, exact-LINE→asymptotic-MLE, coef in original units → e^β = odds ratio, CI exponentiates. Read the table the same way.

Slide 38 (Framingham): ~3 min. Forest plot. Age, smoking, sysBP, glucose all have OR > 1 with CIs excluding 1. BMI’s CI crosses 1 (not distinguishable from no effect, given the other predictors). Walk age concretely: standardized OR ≈ 1.78 per 1 SD (~9 years); 50→60 multiplies CHD odds by ~1.95.

Closing (4 min) — slides 39–41

Slide 39 (summary): ~2 min. Five takeaways: Airbnb act-on-it, NBA don’t-act-on-it, same machinery → check effect size not just p-value, diagnostics catch statistical not systematic, logistic = same template with z.

Slide 40 (next: Ch 12.5): ~1 min. Bootstrap CI for AUC, permutation test for classifier vs random, multiple-testing on logistic coefficients. Same template — only the test statistic and the response variable change. Then Ch 13 opens Act 3 with trees and random forests.

Slide 41 (feedback): ~30 sec. QR code + URL.
