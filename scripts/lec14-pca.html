Block 1: The picture + standardization (~22 min) — slides 6–13

Key narrative

Pearson 1901 + JPM/BAC scatter ground students in the geometry. Then the chapter’s two-stocks-by-hand computation makes the algebra concrete before we scale to 95. The standardization story (raw vs standardized PC1) is the first surprise: feature scaling matters, and skipping it lets TSLA dominate.

Slide 6 (dark transition): “the picture: best line, best subspace.”

Slide 7 (Pearson 1901): ~90 sec. PCA is over a century old. Karl Pearson asked the question in 1901 and answered it in any number of dimensions with elementary geometry. The figure on the left is from his original Phil. Mag. paper: a line \(AB\) through a cloud of points, with perpendicular drops \(p_i\). PCA picks \(AB\) to minimize \(\sum p_i^2\). That’s the whole problem. Eigenvalues and SVD come in as the answer, not the question.

Spend a moment on the figure — students recognize what it shows immediately.

Slide 8 (JPM/BAC scatter): ~2 min. Same Pearson picture, drawn on real data. Each blue dot is one trading day’s pair of returns: JPM on x, BAC on y, 2014–2024. Two big banks; correlation 0.89.

The cloud is elliptical. PC1 (orange) is the long axis — the shared up/down move of the two banks, the “banking sector” direction. PC2 (green) is the short axis — what’s left, the small idiosyncratic difference.

The dotted red line shows one day’s perpendicular drop onto PC1 — its squared length is what PCA minimizes, summed over all days.

Slide 9 (PC1 by hand): ~90 sec. The whole computation in seven lines. Two banks, two PCs, no mystery. - PC1 direction = (+0.65, +0.76) — both positive, both around 0.7. Northeast in the JPM-BAC plane. The “banking sector” direction in numbers. - PC1 variance share = 94.4% of total variance lives along PC1. PC2 picks up the rest. - Example day score = +6.12. The day with JPM up 6%, BAC up 3% projects far out along PC1 — almost the whole move is sector co-movement.

Scaling to 95 stocks doesn’t change the recipe — only the size of the matrix.

Slide 10 (scaling up): ~30 sec. The 95-stock dataset. 2,770 trading days × 95 surviving S&P 100 names. Same eigendecomposition, larger matrix.

Slide 11 (DISCUSSION: poll — predict which stocks dominate without standardization): 2 min. POLL. Four options: - (a) the largest companies (Apple, Microsoft, Berkshire) - (b) the most volatile stocks (Tesla, semiconductors) - (c) the financials (banks, insurance) - (d) PC1 will be roughly equal across all 95

Target: (b). PCA finds whichever direction has the largest raw variance. Tesla swings 5% on a slow day; KO barely moves 1%. Without standardization, the high-vol names dominate.

If a student picks (a): natural confusion (large cap → large index weight). PCA doesn’t see market caps, just variance. If they pick (d): correct after standardization, not before.

Slide 12 (without standardization — the contrast plot): ~2 min. Both panels show PC1 loadings sorted. Left (raw): steep nose, long flat tail. Top five most volatile names hold ~12% of PC1’s squared mass — over 2× the uniform baseline (5/95 ≈ 5.3%). PC1 is shaped by “TSLA and the other big movers”. Right (standardized): broad, uniformly positive bar. Every stock contributes nearly equally. Same data, same algorithm — different scale.

Slide 13 (the fix — standardize): ~1 min. Definition box for z-score. After standardization, PCA finds structure in the correlations instead of the magnitudes. For returns, this is almost always what we want. Mathematical statement: PCA on raw centered data decomposes the covariance matrix; PCA on standardized data decomposes the correlation matrix.

Block 2: Reading the components + the math (~22 min) — slides 14–22

Key narrative

Scree plot, named-factor reveal (PC1/2/3), then the variational + SVD math. End the block with a discussion that surfaces the chapter’s central methodological lesson — interpretability is not validation.

Slide 14 (dark transition): “reading the components.”

Slide 15 (scree plot): ~90 sec. Left: bar chart, PC1 alone is ~40% of variance, sharp drop, long flat tail. Right: cumulative variance — 70% takes ~20 components, 80% takes ~35. A naive elbow read picks \(k=1\). Hold that — block 3 will show CV picks \(k=3\) instead. Different objectives, different answers.

The shape (“one big factor + long tail”) is the textbook signature of equity returns: market factor swamps everything, sector/style factors are smaller but real, the rest is idiosyncratic.

Slide 16 (elbow method — heuristic, not formula): ~1 min. Definition box for elbow method. The chapter’s empirical demo: scree elbow says \(k=1\), CV picks \(k=3\). The elbow ranks PCs by training variance — PC1 swamps the others. CV ranks by held-out portfolio variance — PC2/PC3 stabilize the covariance estimate enough to matter.

Moral: pick \(k\) by what wins on the task you care about.

Slide 17 (interpret PC1 — the market factor): ~2 min. Each PC is a vector of weights — the loadings.

Top panel (PC1): every loading is positive. A stock’s PC1 loading = its sensitivity to the market. PC1 is the textbook market factor.

Bottom panel (PC2, colored by GICS sector): sharp pattern. Purple bars (Financials — JPM, BAC, WFC, C, COF) positive. Red bars (Energy) positive. Green (Staples — PG, KO) and dark blue (Utilities) negative. PC2 is the cyclical-vs-defensive contrast. Cyclicals rally when economy strong; investors flee to staples/utilities for safety.

PCA discovered this without anyone naming sectors. The factors fell out of the eigendecomposition.

Slide 18 (interpret PC3 — growth vs yield): ~1 min. Top loaders: Nvidia, Amazon, Meta, Adobe, Alphabet — big-tech growth. Bottom: utilities (DUK, SO), staples (KO), high-dividend telecom (VZ) — yield/income. By PC5 or PC6 the loadings stop telling crisp economic stories.

Slide 19 (DISCUSSION: did PCA work?): 3 min think-pair-share (1 min think + 2 min pair). Set up the central methodological lesson.

Target answer: no. Beautifully named factors are appealing — they pass the human-narrative test — but they’re not validation. The factors named themselves because we already know the universe. We need a method-agnostic test of whether the components carry useful information.

The chapter’s answer (block 3): use the components on a downstream task and evaluate on data we never used to fit them. Interpretability is not validation.

If a student says “yes, the named factors prove it” — that’s exactly the position the chapter argues against. Hold and let block 3 unsettle it.

Slide 20 (the math — variational form): ~90 sec. Among all \(k\)-dim subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^p\), PCA picks the one closest to the data. By Pythagoras (after centering): minimizing residual = maximizing projected variance.

Continuity with Pearson: \(k=1\) is Pearson’s line; \(k=2\) is a plane; for our stocks, \(k=3\) is a 3D subspace inside \(\mathbb{R}^{95}\).

Slide 21 (the math — SVD): ~90 sec. The variational form said what; SVD says how. Every \(n \times p\) matrix factors as \(X = USV^T\). First \(k\) columns of \(V\) are PC1, …, PC\(k\). Variance of PC\(_i\) = \(\sigma_i^2 / (n-1)\).

Bottom table is a cheat sheet for students flipping between sklearn docs, finance literature, and our notation. Three communities, three names for the same objects. Don’t dwell — point and move on.

Slide 22 (verify): ~1 min. Five lines of code: SVD components match sklearn’s .components_ up to a sign per PC. Differences at \(10^{-15}\) to \(10^{-17}\) are floating-point noise. The two routines compute the same object, packaged differently.

Worthwhile habit: when a textbook says “X is just Y”, verify on real data.

Block 3: The payoff + cautionary tale (~28 min) — slides 23–33

Key narrative

The reason PCA is in this course: it solves a concrete portfolio problem when the standard formula breaks (\(p > n\)). The walk-forward backtest is the validation that delivers on block 2’s setup — held-out portfolio variance is the proof, not the named factors. End with the Novembre cautionary tale: a beautiful PCA figure isn’t always discovery.

Slide 23 (dark transition): “the payoff: portfolios in the \(p > n\) regime.”

Slide 24 (why minimize variance — shouldn’t we maximize return?): ~90 sec. Address the obvious student objection up front. High variance kills long-run wealth.

Walk through the 50%/50% example: $1 → $0.50 → $0.75. Down 25%, not flat. Compounding penalizes losses more than gains. General statement: \(E[\log(1+r)] \approx \mu - \sigma^2/2\) — variance subtracts directly from compound growth (“volatility drag”).

Practical: low-volatility ETFs (USMV, SPLV) are billion-dollar funds. Pension funds and risk-parity strategies are explicitly variance-minimizing. So minimum-variance isn’t a quirky academic objective — it’s a major chunk of how real money invests.

The parenthetical at the bottom acknowledges: full Markowitz trades off return AND variance. We focus on variance because PCA helps estimate covariance.

Slide 25 (Markowitz — minimum-variance portfolio): ~90 sec. Two-line problem: - Variance of the portfolio’s return is \(w^T \Sigma w\) - Constraint: weights sum to 1 - Solution: \(w \propto \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}\)

Read the inverse-row-sum interpretation: low-vol stocks and natural hedges get larger weights; volatile and correlated stocks get smaller. So we need \(\Sigma^{-1}\). Where do we get \(\Sigma\)? From a recent window of historical returns.

Slide 26 (the failure mode — \(p > n\)): ~1 min. The crisis. 95 stocks, 60 days. The 95×95 sample covariance from 60 observations has rank at most 59 — singular, inverse doesn’t exist.

Adding a small ridge makes it invertible but unstable: weights enormous, change wildly with one-day window shifts. Useless. The math we were taught for \(\Sigma^{-1}\) assumed \(n \gg p\). In high-dim finance, that’s rarely true.

Slide 27 (PCA gives us \(\Sigma\) — factor model): ~90 sec. Instead of estimating the full \(95 \times 95\) from 60 days, constrain it: \(k\) pervasive factors + idiosyncratic noise.

Factor part \(V_k \Lambda_k V_k^T\) captures systematic risk. Diagonal \(D\) accounts for stock-specific risk and makes the estimate positive definite (so the inverse exists).

Catch: we have to pick \(k\). Scree elbow says \(k=1\). CV will say \(k=3\).

Slide 28 (the four estimators): ~90 sec. Equal-weight (baseline), ridge sample (invertibility hack), random projection (sanity check — does PCA’s variance alignment matter?), PCA factor (the contender).

Walk-forward evaluation: train on past 60 days, hold for next 21, slide. Time-respecting CV. Random 5-fold would leak future regimes into the past.

Slide 29 (the result — variance vs \(k\)): ~2 min. Blue (PCA): drops sharply 1→3, flat 3→10, rises past 10. Same bias-variance trade-off as in regression, but for covariance estimation. Gray (random projection): above PCA throughout — variance-aligned directions matter. Red (ridge): worst. Green (equal-weight): beats ridge but lags PCA.

CV picks \(k=3\). Three factors capture the systematic structure; more starts adding noise.

Slide 30 (the result — cumulative variance over time): ~90 sec. PCA factor portfolio sits lowest the entire test period. COVID jump in early 2020: every line shoots up, but PCA’s jump is the smallest of the four. The market’s structure changed fast in March 2020, and a factor-model estimate adapted faster than the alternatives.

Slide 31 (interpretability is not validation): ~1 min. The chapter’s central methodological lesson, and the answer to slide 19’s discussion. The named factors looked impressive, but a named factor is just a story. The proof comes from a downstream task evaluated on data we never used to fit the components.

Eval-by-downstream-task: same idea as cross-validation in regression.

Slide 32 (cautionary tale — genes mirror geography): ~2 min. Novembre et al. ran PCA on ~197K SNPs across 1,387 Europeans, gave the algorithm zero geographic info, and the first two principal components reproduced the map of Europe. PC1 lined up with latitude, PC2 with longitude.

The temptation: read this as “PCA discovered hidden European population structure.” The same research community published the math showing otherwise: Novembre & Stephens (2008, Nat. Gen.) proved that PCA on any spatial data with distance-decaying similarity will produce gradient/sinusoidal patterns generically — no historical demographic event required.

So the famous figure was always going to come out — the math made it inevitable given the sampling. The structure was built into the input geography, not discovered as European prehistory.

The lesson — and a parallel to today’s named-factor discussion: a PCA figure that reproduces something obvious about the data (a map, a known taxonomy) is not by itself evidence that PCA discovered hidden structure. Same lesson next lecture, on clustering. Genes mirror geography → PredPol mirrors policing → MBTI mirrors cutpoints. Three fields, same artifact.

Slide 33 (DISCUSSION: PCA or pick five?): 3 min think-pair-share (1 min think + 2 min pair).

Target answers: - Lasso is right when the truth is sparse — only a handful of features matter, the rest are noise. Example: predict next-quarter earnings from one of 200 specific accounting ratios. You want a short list. - PCA is right when many features carry useful information and you want a low-dim summary that pools them. Example: today’s covariance matrix where every stock is partly driven by a common market factor. A sparse subset would miss the market hitting all of them. - Third option — sparse PCA — splits the difference: PCs in which most loadings are zero.

Deeper point: PCA and Lasso answer different questions. Lasso says “which features matter?” PCA says “what’s a low-dim summary?”

Common questions and how to handle them

“Why log returns instead of simple returns?” — Log returns add cleanly across time (chapter line at first use). For small daily moves they’re nearly indistinguishable from simple percent returns. Conventional in finance.

“Why does PCA need standardization but k-means tomorrow?” — Same reason: both use Euclidean distance / variance, both let the largest-magnitude feature dominate without standardization. Same fix (z-score). Lec15 will hit this again.

“How do we interpret negative PC2 loadings?” — A PC’s sign is arbitrary (we could flip PC2 → -PC2 and the math is identical). What matters is the contrast — which stocks land on opposite sides. We chose the sign convention so financials are positive and staples are negative.

“Why is PC1’s variance share only ~40%, not higher?” — Because we standardized. Before standardization, TSLA’s huge raw variance dominated, inflating the first PC’s share. After standardization, every stock contributes roughly equally to total variance, and the market factor only captures the correlated part — about 40% on this universe.

“What if I want both factors AND a return objective?” — That’s the full Markowitz mean-variance frontier. We focus on the variance-only problem because covariance estimation is where PCA bites. Once you have a stable \(\hat\Sigma\), plugging into the full mean-variance problem is straightforward (and you need an expected-return estimate, which is its own hard problem).

“How does this scale to 5,000 stocks?” — Same recipe. SVD scales to large matrices; randomized SVD makes very large problems tractable. Real risk models in industry (Barra, Axioma) are essentially fancier factor models with thousands of assets and dozens of factors — still PCA’s variance-aligned-directions idea.
