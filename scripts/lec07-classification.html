Lecture 7: Classification — Facilitation Script

Duration: 75 minutes Primary dataset: Framingham Heart Study (CHD, 15% base rate) Hook: 85% accuracy model that catches zero patients Structure: Opening (5 min) → Block 1: Mechanics (~20 min) → Block 2: Accuracy trap (~15 min + 5 min poll) → Block 3: Thresholds (~10 min + 5 min poll) → Block 4: Subgroups + calibration (~10 min + 5 min poll) → Closing (2 min) → Buffer

Opening (5 min)

Slides 1–2 (dark hook + readmissions setup). Open with the drama: the model was 85% accurate and caught zero. Pause. Then the readmissions setup — base rate is 15%, null-model baseline looks like a good model. Key: this is not a toy problem. CMS penalties are real; hospitals actually deploy models like this.

Slide 3 (agenda): Four blocks. Mechanics → accuracy trap → thresholds → what aggregates hide.

Block 1: Mechanics (~20 min) — slides 4–12

Key narrative

We need a classifier. Naive OLS fails because predictions leave [0, 1]. Sigmoid trick → logistic regression. Fit with gradient descent on a convex loss.

Slide 4 (binary outcome): Introduce Framingham. 4,240 patients, 10-year followup. Outcome is 0/1. Stake the vocabulary: CHD = 1 = positive class; no CHD = 0 = negative class. Positive = detected, not desirable — the positive CHD test is bad news. This matters when we talk sensitivity/specificity later.

Slide 5 (linreg fails): Show the scatter. Predictions below 0 and above 1. “You can’t use OLS because nothing constrains predictions to [0, 1].”

Slide 6 (sigmoid): “Rather than constrain OLS, transform what we model.” Push z through σ. Show the S-shape. z = 0 maps to p = 0.5.

Slide 7 (log-odds table): This is the same idea from a different angle. Walk the rows. Log-odds range from −∞ to +∞ — exactly what a linear model can output. Logistic regression = linear model of log-odds.

Slide 8 (Framingham age fit): “We see the lower portion of the S because the base rate is low.” Age alone doesn’t get anyone above 30% predicted risk.

Slide 9 (logistic loss formula): No closed form. Pick a loss. Emphasize the confident wrong penalty.

Slide 10 (loss comparison): Visual — squared stays bounded, logistic explodes. This is the asymmetry that forces the model to avoid overconfidence on hard cases.

Slide 11 (gradient descent): Hiking metaphor. ∇L = uphill, step the opposite way. η = step size. Convex = single bowl (assuming data isn’t separable — don’t belabor, just note).

Slide 12 (GD convergence): Loss drops fast, settles. This is 50 iterations on a 2-feature model; sklearn uses a fancier variant (L-BFGS).

Block 2: The Accuracy Trap (~15 min + 5 min poll) — slides 13–20

Key narrative

Fit the model, compute accuracy, celebrate → reveal the baseline is identical → unmask accuracy as misleading under class imbalance → introduce confusion matrix, precision, recall.

Slide 13 (dark): Block break.

Slide 14 (fit + test accuracy): 85.5%. Hold it in mind.

Slide 15 (baseline reveal): “Always predict no CHD” scores 84.8%. Our model improves by 0.7 points. This is the lecture’s most important beat — land it slowly.

Slide 16 (accuracy trap callout): General principle. Accuracy is population-averaged. With imbalance, the common class dominates.

Slide 17 (confusion matrix): Walk through the four cells. TN dominates. Bottom row is where we care about — only 17 of 167 CHD cases caught. “Accuracy hid this because there are so many true negatives.”

Slide 18 (precision vs recall definitions): Two metrics, two questions. Precision = “trust the flag?” Recall = “of all the sick, how many did we find?”

Slide 19 (our P and R): 63% precision, 8% recall. A disaster for a screening tool.

Slide 20 (discussion poll): FP vs FN costs — $500 vs tens of thousands. Students commit. Then flip: spam filter reverses the costs. Key lesson: the threshold is a cost-benefit call, not a statistical one. 2 minutes max. Don’t resolve — transition to Block 3.

Block 3: Threshold Choice (~10 min) — slides 21–24

Key narrative

Sweep thresholds. ROC for ranking; PR for tradeoff; actual metrics at 5 thresholds to make it concrete.

Slide 21 (dark): Block break.

Slide 22 (ROC): Perfect model hugs top-left. Ours is 0.75 AUC. Concordance probability — “pick one CHD, one non-CHD, model ranks correctly 75% of the time.” Clean, threshold-free.

Slide 23 (PR curve): Same information, different view. No dominant point — you must choose.

Slide 24 (threshold effect bars): Concrete numbers at 5 thresholds. Low threshold → catch more, trust each flag less.

Block 4: What Aggregates Hide (~15 min + 5 min poll) — slides 25–33

Key narrative

Three ways a “good” aggregate metric can mask disaster: base rates, subgroups, calibration.

Slide 25 (dark): Block break.

Slide 26 (base rate callout): Set up the 99% claim. Pause. Ask students to commit to an answer before the reveal.

Slide 27 (10,000 patient visualization): Walk through the arithmetic. 50 cancer cases + 100 false positives = ~33% posterior. “The ‘99% accuracy’ was true; the patient’s P(cancer | positive) is 33%.” Base rate neglect. This is why mass screening programs are controversial.

Slide 28 (subgroup discussion poll): Let students predict which group the model fails for. Most will say “young patients” because age is the dominant feature. Good — set up the reveal. 2 minutes max.

Slide 29 (subgroup AUC): Reveal. Under-40 AUC ≈ 0.36. Below 0.5 = worse than random. Inverting the ranking would do better. Caveat: only ~6 CHD cases in 191 patients, so the estimate is noisy — but the direction is real. The model learned “older = sicker” and nothing else.

Slide 30 (boxplot): Same failure in a different view. Young: everything bunched at zero. Old: red above blue. The model is essentially blind to young-patient risk.

Slide 31 (calibration plot): New question. Do probabilities mean what they say? Decile bins → observed vs predicted. Our model tracks the diagonal reasonably well. It’s well-calibrated, despite the ranking failures above. These are different properties.

Slide 32 (AUC vs calibration): Two properties. AUC = ranking, invariant to monotone rescaling. Calibration = absolute values. Which matters depends on how you use the output.

Closing (2 min) — slides 33–35

Slide 33 (dark): Closing loop.

Slide 34 (back to readmissions): Return to the opening hook. Now students know what to ask: baseline, precision/recall, ROC, subgroups, calibration. The operating-point choice is a cost-benefit decision, not a statistical one.

Slide 35 (takeaways): Six bullets. Reveal incrementally.

Slide 36 (next time): Forward pointers. Act 2 opens with bootstrap, which gives CIs on today’s metrics. Ch 12 does formal inference on logistic coefficients. Ch 13 adds trees.

Timing flexibility

If running short (< 60 min available): - Cut the GD convergence slide (slide 12); just mention it. - Skip the PR curve (slide 23); threshold bars (slide 24) cover the same tradeoff. - Shorten subgroup block to just the AUC bar chart (slide 29); skip boxplot.

If running long (> 80 min): - Add a worked confusion matrix exercise after slide 17: “if the threshold is 0.3, fill in the cells.” - Expand the base rate neglect exercise: have students compute for 0.1% prevalence, then 1%, then 10%, and graph P(cancer | positive) vs prevalence.

Discussion / poll cues

  1. Slide 20 — FP vs FN tradeoff. Commit cards or PollEv. 2 minutes. Goal: students internalize that the tradeoff is context-dependent.
  2. Slide 28 — which subgroup fails? Think-pair. 2 minutes. Goal: students anticipate the reveal, which makes slide 29 land harder.

Vignettes / callbacks

  • Readmissions hook (opening + closing): ties statistics to a real medical operations problem. CMS HRRP penalties are real — up to 3% of Medicare reimbursements.
  • Framingham (throughout): 1948 start, 3 generations followed. Named after Framingham, Massachusetts. Identified cholesterol, BP, and smoking as cardiovascular risk factors.
  • 99% accurate cancer test (base rate): a stand-in for mammography critiques — high sensitivity and specificity, low prevalence, and low positive predictive value. Real debate: 2009 USPSTF recommendation against annual mammography under 50 was driven by exactly this math.
