Lecture 15: Clustering — K-Means — Facilitation Script

Duration: 80 minutes Total slides: 39 (slide 1 = auto title from YAML) Primary dataset: Airbnb NYC listings (~29,000 listings after dropna) Hook: Airbnb host wants a competitive set so they can price against comparable listings. Today: when you ask k-means for one, do you get the answer or your own assumptions back? Headline: Your features pick your clusters. Two surprises: (1) drop lat/lon and the clusters flip from geography to market segments; (2) different random seeds produce different clusters (two of 10 runs disagree at ARI ≈ 0.72-0.76). Aphorism: all models are wrong, but some are useful — George Box. Structure: Opening (~3 min, slides 1–5) → Block 1: algorithm + first run (~22 min, slides 6–14) → Block 2: validation tools + their limits (~22 min, slides 15–23) → Block 3: features pick your clusters (~26 min, slides 24–35) → Closing (~3 min, slides 36–39) → Buffer (~4 min)

Block 1: Algorithm + first run (~22 min) — slides 6–14

Key narrative

Ground students in the k-means mechanics, then run it once on the full Airbnb feature set. The result — clusters that mostly trace NYC geography — is the deliberate anti-climax that sets up the whole rest of the lecture.

Slide 6 (dark transition): “assign, recompute, repeat.”

Slide 7 (the data): ~1 min. Toy table of five Airbnb listings. The pedagogical move: an unsupervised problem looks identical to a supervised one except there’s no \(y\) column. Quick note that room_type needs one-hot encoding for distance-based methods.

Slide 8 (k-means in four steps): ~1 min. Definition box: assign → recompute → repeat until stable. Plus three glosses: centroid = cluster mean, starts from \(k\) random centroids, converges in finite steps (finitely many assignments, monotone objective).

Slide 9 (watch it iterate): ~1 min. The 4-panel chapter figure — k-means on (price, reviews) standardized, \(k=3\). Panel 1 random init, panel 4 converged. Walk the eye through one cycle: every point picks its nearest X, then each X moves to the mean of its neighborhood. Two features only so we can plot it.

Slide 10 (the objective): ~90 sec. Definition box with inertia: \(\sum_k \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2\). Gloss below the formula. Two key properties: 1. Each step monotonically decreases (assign picks nearest; recompute uses the mean, which minimizes squared distance). 2. Non-convex — different starts can land at different local minima. Foreshadows block 2.

Slide 11 (one more thing first — standardize): ~1 min. K-means uses Euclidean distance, so units matter. Tiny table — price (range 10–10,000) vs. latitude (range 40.5–40.9). Without standardization, price drowns out everything. Same lesson as PCA on Wednesday. StandardScaler does the symmetric thing.

Slide 12 (run it — all the features): ~75 sec. Eight numerical features + four one-hot room_type columns. Standardize, fit, predict. Cluster sizes: 13,442 / 651 / 15,049 — two big clusters and one tiny ~650-row cluster (could be niche or could be noise). random_state=42, n_init=10 (more on n_init in block 2).

Slide 13 (look at the clusters): ~1 min. NYC map colored by cluster. Cluster 2 (green) blankets Manhattan/Brooklyn/much of Queens, the other two scattered. Tagline: “they trace geography. is that useful?” Answer (don’t say it yet): not really — we already have a neighbourhood_group_cleansed column. The algorithm spent its effort rediscovering boroughs (badly).

Slide 14 (DISCUSSION: poll — first question to ask): 2 min. POLL. Four options: - is \(k=3\) right? - are clusters stable? - what do they represent? - would different features give different clusters?

All four are correct. The lecture answers them in order — k+stability in block 2, representation+features in block 3. Whatever students pick is fine. One-line debrief: “all four are necessary. clustering looks easy because it runs. validating it is the hard part.”

Block 2: Validation tools + their limits (~22 min) — slides 15–23

Key narrative

The two standard heuristics (elbow + silhouette) plus seed stability — none oracle the right answer. Students leave the block knowing that the validation toolbox is for ruling out bad choices, not for picking the right one.

Slide 15 (dark transition): “choose k. check stability. neither will save you.”

Slide 16 (elbow + silhouette: the standard tools): ~90 sec. Two stacked definition boxes. Elbow = plot inertia vs. \(k\), look for a bend. Silhouette = per-point fit-to-own-cluster vs. nearest-other-cluster, range \([-1, 1]\), averaged. These are the heuristics every clustering tutorial reaches for.

Slide 17 (elbow plot — no bend): ~2 min. The chapter’s elbow + silhouette plots (side-by-side). Inertia drops smoothly from ~245k at \(k=2\) to ~120k at \(k=10\) — no bend. Silhouette peaks at \(k=2\) (~0.30) — the most trivial split, just half. The two heuristics disagree, and neither is decisive. Choosing \(k\) is a judgment call, not a formula. (Heuristics CAN rule out clearly bad choices — silhouette crashing toward 0 at \(k=15\) tells you not to use 15.)

Style-guide note: this plot should ideally be stacked vertically (shared x-axis). The chapter renders it side-by-side. If revising the chapter later, stack to a single column.

Slide 18 (DISCUSSION: poll — same data, same k, different runs?): 2 min. POLL. Four options, set up the seed-instability reveal. - (a) always — deterministic - (b) usually - (c) sometimes — depends on data - (d) rarely

Target: (c). Most pairs agree, but a meaningful fraction disagree by a lot. Reveal on slide 20. If anyone picks (a): “let’s check.”

Slide 19 (different seeds, different clusters): ~1 min. ARI definition box. Bullet list: ARI=1 → identical, ARI=0 → random agreement. For any pair of points, do both runs co-cluster them the same way? Adjusted for chance.

Slide 20 (10 runs, pairwise ARI): ~90 sec. The ARI heatmap. Most cells dark red (ARI = 1.00), runs 3 and 7 sit at 0.72-0.76 against everyone else — they found different local minima. Same data, same algorithm, same \(k\) → not the same clusters. This is real-life k-means: most starts converge to the same answer, a few find others, and you can’t tell from the output which is “right.”

Slide 21 (sklearn’s defenses): ~1 min. n_init=10 keeps the lowest-inertia of 10 random starts. k-means++ initialization spreads initial centroids apart (algorithmic: probability of picking each centroid proportional to squared distance from the nearest existing one). The earlier heatmap used n_init=1 to expose the raw instability. With n_init=10, fewer disagreements — but the underlying multi-modal landscape remains.

Slide 22 (what does “Cluster 1” mean?): ~1 min. Warning callout — if clusters shift with the seed, the integer labels carry no portable meaning. “Cluster 1” in one run is not “Cluster 1” in the next. Never report integer cluster labels — report profiles (budget, moderate, premium). This rule lands in block 3.

Slide 23 (DISCUSSION: you have to ship something. it’s monday.): 5 min think-pair-share (1 min think + 2 min pair + 2 min debrief). Tight constraint: elbow gradual, silhouette peaks at \(k=2\), two of 10 runs disagree, host wants three segments by Friday. Name two concrete things to do before reporting.

Collect any 2 of: 1. n_init=50+ with fixed seed — buys stability for compute. 2. Profile every cluster — mean feature values, name them. 3. Try a different feature set — sets up block 3. 4. Visualize on 2D slices — price-reviews, lat-lon, accommodates-price. 5. Three tiers might be what the host needs regardless of the math. 6. Drop features you don’t trust — foreshadows block 3.

Key insight: clustering is a judgment call you defend with profiles, not a formula you compute. If a pair says “give up and use random forest” — reframe: clustering is descriptive, not predictive. We’re summarizing structure, not predicting anything.

Block 3: Features pick your clusters (~26 min) — slides 24–35

Key narrative

The chapter’s headline result, plus three real-world cases that show the same lesson in genetics, policing, and personality testing. Closing: the clustering gotcha checklist — six questions to ask before reporting any cluster.

Slide 24 (dark transition): “your features pick your clusters.”

Slide 25 (the same data, two feature sets): 30 sec. Frame the experiment: drop just lat/lon, everything else identical. Same \(k\), same standardization, same algorithm. Two columns out of ten — how much can it change? Predict-then-reveal setup.

Slide 26 (the reveal): ~2 min. THE CENTRAL FIGURE. Two NYC maps, both colored by cluster, both \(k=3\), both standardized. Left (with lat/lon): clusters trace geography — Manhattan/Brooklyn dominance. Right (without lat/lon): same colors mix across the same map. Geography is no longer the axis — the clusters now organize around price, room type, and size. Block 2’s elbow plot and silhouette wouldn’t have told you which to prefer. Let the slide sit; this is the answer to Monday’s question.

Slide 27 (what the right-side clusters actually capture): ~90 sec. Profile table for the no-lat/lon run. - Cluster 0 (13,438): private room, ~$77/night, 1.1 bedrooms → moderate - Cluster 1 (15,053): entire home/apt, ~$186/night, 1.9 bedrooms → premium - Cluster 2 (651): shared room, ~$57/night, 0.4 bedrooms → budget

Three competitive sets a host can actually price against. Reviews per listing are roughly similar; the split is room type and price. These are the useful clusters.

Slide 28 (why did lat/lon dominate?): ~1 min. Two-column lat/lon → spatial separation is strong and clean. A Manhattan listing and a Bronx listing are several standard deviations apart in (lat, lon) after standardization — that swamps the more subtle separations in price, room type, etc. Moral: any feature with clean geometric separation will dominate the cluster boundaries.

Slide 29 (DISCUSSION: which features would you drop?): 5 min (2 think + 2 pair + 1 debrief). For each feature, decide keep / drop / it depends, in one sentence: - latitude, longitude → drop (we just saw why). - neighbourhood_group_cleansed → drop (discrete geography, same problem). - host_id → drop (identifier, not a feature; creates one-cluster-per-host). - last_review_date → it depends (could mean activity, could mean listing age; convert to days-since). - description text length → it depends (could proxy effort/quality, could be noise; worth a robustness pass).

Key insight: feature selection IS the cluster-design step. The algorithm doesn’t decide; you do. Defend each feature by what segment-relevant information it carries.

Slide 30 (genes mirror geography): ~2 min. Novembre et al., Nature 2008. PCA on ~197K SNPs from 1,387 Europeans — no geographic info handed in. Result: PC1 vs PC2 plot reproduces the map of Europe. PC1-vs-latitude \(r^2 = 0.71\). 50% of people placeable within 310 km of birthplace. Same research community (Novembre & Stephens 2008, Nature Genetics) published the math showing these patterns are generic to PCA on spatial data with distance-decaying similarity — no demographic event required. The Cavalli-Sforza interpretation of European PC1 as “Neolithic farmers walking west” is at minimum unidentified by this method.

Consequence: these maps are misappropriated to support biological-race claims that the authors themselves reject. Class line: “We gave the algorithm DNA, with zero geographic info. It drew us a map. Did it discover hidden populations? Or did it rediscover the obvious — your neighbors share more ancestry with you than strangers do? The math says the second.”

Slide 31 (predictive policing finds the police): ~2 min. Lum & Isaac, Significance 2016. Reimplemented PredPol on Oakland 2010 drug arrest data. The algorithm’s only feature was where past arrests happened. Hotspots: West Oakland + International Blvd, ~200× more arrests than non-hotspot areas. Compared to estimated true drug use (NSDUH + Census): roughly uniform across Oakland. The clusters traced arrest patterns, not crime patterns. Quantitative consequence: Black residents face ~2× the policing exposure of white residents under PredPol, despite roughly even drug use.

Policy consequence: Santa Cruz banned predictive policing in 2020 (first US city), LAPD ended its contract. Central citation in AI-fairness debates.

Class line: “Same algorithm, two feature sets. Train on arrests, you get a map of where we already police. Train on a public-health survey, you get a map of where drugs are. Different maps. The choice between them is a policy decision in statistics clothing.”

Slide 32 (sixteen personality types — or four coin-flips at the median): ~2 min. McCrae & Costa 1989; Bess & Harvey 2002. MBTI assigns 16 types via four binary axes (E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P), cutting each continuous trait at zero. The underlying scores are roughly normal with the mode AT the cutpoint. Earlier “bimodality” reports (Bess & Harvey) were BILOG quadrature artifacts. About 50% of takers get a different type on retest within weeks. The clusters exist because the test designer wrote them in.

Industry consequence: ~$20M/year, 89 of the Fortune 100 use MBTI in hiring/team-building/career counseling. Hiring decisions made on a clustering structure the data doesn’t have.

Class line: “Sixteen personality types sounds like a discovery. It’s not. It’s four coin-flips at the median of a bell curve. The ‘clusters’ are a printing decision, not a biological fact.”

Slide 33 (same lesson, three fields): ~1 min. Synthesis. Genetics → geography. Policing → policing. Psychology → 16 boxes. Airbnb → boroughs. Clustering’s first job is to mirror its inputs, not to discover something independent of them. This is the deepest version of the lesson. Students should leave with the instinct: before trusting any cluster summary, ask what features were fed in and what they encode.

Slide 34 (the clustering gotcha checklist): ~90 sec. Six-item checklist — incremental reveal one at a time: - what features did I include? — geography, IDs, dates often dominate - what features did I drop, and why? — feature choice is the model - is \(k\) defensible? — elbow + silhouette + business reason - are the clusters stable? — n_init=50, multiple seeds, ARI > 0.9 - can I name each cluster from its profile? — if not, you have an artifact - does the clustering answer a question someone has? — if no, don’t ship

Order matters: features first (biggest lever), then \(k\), then stability, then interpretation, then decision-relevance.

Slide 35 (demo: clustering in the notebook): 30 sec. Colab link + three experiments: toggle lat/lon, sweep n_init, try \(k = 2, 3, 5, 8\). If running short, defer to HW 4.

Closing (~3 min) — slides 36–39

Slide 36 (summary): ~1 min. Five takeaways (incremental). The fifth — your features pick your clusters — is the headline. If a student takes one thing, this is it.

Slide 37 (Box aphorism dark slide): ~15 sec. “All models are wrong, but some are useful.” The clusters aren’t true categories — they’re a simplification. The question is whether the simplification helps someone make a better decision. Yes (no lat/lon, real segments); no (with lat/lon, just boroughs).

Slide 38 (next: when validation isn’t enough): ~45 sec. Forward pointer to Ch 16 (Wednesday). We’ve trained models, tested, validated. What breaks when the data changes after deployment? Three failure modes: temporal leakage, feedback loops, Goodhart. The validation tools we’ve used assume past data looks like future data. What if it doesn’t?

Vignette quick-reference

Novembre et al. (2008), “Genes mirror geography within Europe,” Nature 456:98–101.

~197K SNPs, 1,387 Europeans. PCA. PC1 ~ latitude (\(r^2 = 0.71\)), PC2 ~ longitude (\(r^2 = 0.72\)). 50% of subjects placed within 310 km of reported origin. The companion paper (Novembre & Stephens 2008, Nature Genetics 40:646–649) proves these patterns are generic to PCA on spatial data with distance-decaying similarity — they arise even from pure isolation-by-distance with no demographic event. François et al. 2010 went further: under range expansions, PC1 can orient perpendicular to the true expansion axis. Authors are mainstream pop-gen scientists, not retrofitting critics.

Lum & Isaac (2016), “To predict and serve?” Significance 13(5):14–19.

Replicated PredPol on Oakland 2010 drug arrest data. Only feature: where past arrests happened. Predicted hotspots: West Oakland, International Blvd, ~200× arrest density of non-hotspots. NSDUH-estimated drug use: roughly uniform. Under PredPol, Black residents face ~2× white residents’ policing exposure, other non-white races ~1.5×, despite even drug use. Santa Cruz banned PredPol 2020 (first US city); LAPD ended contract same year.

McCrae & Costa (1989), J. Personality 57(1):17–40; Bess & Harvey (2002), J. Personality Assessment 78(1):176–186.

MBTI: 16 types from four binary axes, dichotomized at zero. McCrae & Costa: scores roughly normal with mode at cutpoint. Bess & Harvey: earlier “bimodality” reports were BILOG quadrature artifacts. ~50% retest reclassification within weeks. ~$20M/year industry; 89 of Fortune 100 use it in hiring.
